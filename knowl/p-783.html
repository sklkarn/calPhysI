Consider a disk of radius \(R\) and mass \(M\) is rotating about an axis passing through its center of mass and perpendicular to its plane [FigureÂ 7.1.6.(a)]. To find the moment of inertia of such a disk consider an elementary ring of radius \(r\text{,}\) thickness \(\,dx\text{,}\) and mass \(\,dm\) then the moment of inertia of this elementary ring (annulus) is given by
\begin{equation*} \,dI = \,dm r^{2} \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad I = \int \,dI = \int\limits_{0}^{R}\left(\frac{M}{\pi R^{2}}(2\pi r\,dr)\right)r^{2} \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \because \text{mass of a ring} =(\text{mass per unit area of a disk})\cdot (\text{area of a ring}) \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad I = \frac{2M}{R^{2}}\int\limits_{0}^{R}\left(r^{3}\,dr\right) \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} =\frac{2M}{R^{2}}\left.\frac{r^{4}}{4}\right\vert_{0}^{R} \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} =\frac{M}{2R^{2}}(R^{4}-0)=\frac{1}{2}MR^{2} \end{equation*}
