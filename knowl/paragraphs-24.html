Derivation of Kepler’s I law.

\begin{equation*} F=ma=-\frac{GMm}{r^{2}} \end{equation*}
\begin{equation} \text{or,}\quad \ddot{r}-r\dot{\theta}^{2}=-\frac{GM}{r^{2}} \tag{6.2.3} \end{equation}
\begin{equation*} x=r\cos\theta, \qquad y=r\sin\theta \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \dot{x}=\dot{r}\cos\theta -r\sin\theta\dot{\theta}, \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \dot{y}=\dot{r}\sin\theta +r\cos\theta\dot{\theta} \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} v^{2}=\dot{x}^{2}+\dot{y}^{2} = \dot{r}^{2}+r^{2}\dot{\theta}^{2} \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \ddot{x}=\ddot{r}\cos\theta -\dot{r}\dot{\theta}\sin\theta -\dot{r}\dot{\theta}\sin\theta -r\dot{\theta}^{2}\cos\theta-r\ddot{\theta}\sin\theta, \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \ddot{y}=\ddot{r}\sin\theta +\dot{r}\dot{\theta}\cos\theta +\dot{r}\dot{\theta}\cos\theta -r\dot{\theta}^{2}\sin\theta+r\ddot{\theta}\cos\theta \end{equation*}
Now,
\begin{align*} a\amp =\ddot{x}\cos\theta+\ddot{y}\sin\theta \\ \amp =(\ddot{r}\cos^{2}\theta -2\dot{r}\dot{\theta}\sin\theta\cos\theta -r\dot{\theta}^{2}\cos^{2}\theta-r\ddot{\theta}\sin\theta\cos\theta) \\ \amp +(\ddot{r}\sin^{2}\theta +2\dot{r}\dot{\theta}\sin\theta\cos\theta -r\dot{\theta}^{2}\sin^{2}\theta+r\ddot{\theta}\sin\theta\cos\theta) \\ \amp =\ddot{r}-r\dot{\theta}^{2} \end{align*}
Let
\begin{equation*} \frac{1}{r}=u \end{equation*}
so that
\begin{equation*} \dot{r}=-r^{2}\dot{u} =-r^{2}\frac{\,du}{\,d\theta}\dot{\theta} \end{equation*}
Also,
\begin{equation*} L=mr^{2}\dot{\theta}\quad \Rightarrow\quad \dot{\theta}= \frac{L}{mr^{2}}=hu^{2} \end{equation*}
Now,
\begin{equation*} \dot{r}=-r^{2}\frac{\,du}{\,d\theta} hu^{2}=-h\frac{\,du}{\,d\theta} \end{equation*}
Therefore from eqn (6.2.3),
\begin{equation*} \frac{\,d}{\,dt}\left(\dot{r}\right) -r(hu^{2})^{2}=-GMu^{2} \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad \frac{\,d}{\,dt}\left(-h\frac{\,du}{\,d\theta}\right) -h^{2}u^{3}=-ku^{2} \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad -h\left[\frac{\,d\theta}{\,dt}\frac{\,d}{\,d\theta}\left(\frac{\,du}{\,d\theta}\right)\right] -h^{2}u^{3}=-ku^{2} \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad -h\left[hu^{2}\frac{\,d^{2}u}{\,d\theta^{2}}\right] -h^{2}u^{3}=-ku^{2} \end{equation*}
\begin{equation} \therefore\quad \frac{\,d^{2}u}{\,d\theta^{2}} +u=\frac{k}{h^{2}} \tag{6.2.4} \end{equation}
This is second order linear differential equation with constant coefficient. The solution of which can be given as
\begin{equation*} u=A\cos(\theta+\delta)+\frac{k}{h^{2}} \end{equation*}
It is a solution for a simple harmonic motion equation. Set the coordinate in such a way that \(\delta=0,\) and hence
\begin{equation*} u=\frac{1}{r} = A\cos\theta+\frac{k}{h^{2}} \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad r=\frac{1}{A\cos\theta+\frac{k}{h^{2}}} =\frac{1}{A\cos\theta+\frac{GMm^{2}}{L^{2}}} \end{equation*}
\begin{equation} \therefore\quad r=\frac{1}{\frac{GMm^{2}}{L^{2}}\left(e\cos\theta+1\right)}\tag{6.2.5} \end{equation}
where \(e= \frac{AL^{2}}{GMm^{2}}\) Eqn. (6.2.5) is the general form of an ellipse in polar coordinates, with the origin placed at a focus. In solid state geometry, the parameter \(e\) is often called the eccentricity. When the eccentricity of a planet’s orbit is zero, the orbit is perfectly circular. The curve is an ellipse, a parabola, or a hyperbola, according as its \(e \lt 1, \) \(e = 1, \) or \(e \gt 1. \)
\begin{equation*} \therefore \quad a=\frac{1}{2}\left(r_{min}+r_{max}\right) \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} =\frac{L^{2}}{GMm^{2}}\left(\frac{1}{e+1}+\frac{1}{1-e}\right)= \frac{L^{2}}{GMm^{2}}\left(1-e^{2}\right)^{-1} \end{equation*}
where \(a\) is a semi-major axis of the ellipse, at apoapsis, \(\cos\theta=-1 \) and at periapsis \(\cos\theta=1.\)
