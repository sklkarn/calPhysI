Derivation of Kepler’s II law.

If planet is considered at very close to the semimajor axis, so that point \(P\) is very close to point \(O\text{,}\) then from \(\triangle PSO\) [Figure 6.2.1.(b)], we have -
\begin{equation*} \Delta A =\frac{1}{2}base\times height = \frac{1}{2}(r)(r \Delta \theta) \end{equation*}
where height = \(r\Delta \theta \) and base = \(r\) for the triangle.
\begin{equation*} \therefore \frac{\Delta A}{\Delta t} = \frac{1}{2}r^{2}\frac{\Delta \theta}{\Delta t} =\frac{1}{2}r^{2}\omega \end{equation*}
For small time \(\Delta t\) consider angle subtends by the earth is \(\Delta \theta.\) Now, angular momentum of the planet,
\begin{equation*} L=I\omega = mr^{2}\omega \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \therefore\quad \frac{\Delta A}{\Delta t} =\frac{1}{2}\frac{L}{m} \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \frac{\,dA}{\,dt}=constant \end{equation*}
since angular momentum of a planet in an orbit and its mass remains constant.
